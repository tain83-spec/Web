How I got here

I became a therapist because I had to.

When my relationship with my children's mother broke down, I found myself completely overwhelmed — stressed, confused, unable to understand why I kept ending up in the same painful patterns. I went to therapy, and what I discovered changed everything.

Through my own work, I began to see the roots of what was happening — not just in that relationship, but in how I related to people generally. It was uncomfortable and revealing. But it also opened something: the possibility of genuine connection, of being in a relationship where both people could actually show up as themselves.

That's when I knew I wanted to help others find that same possibility.

Understanding what’s wrong isn’t enough. People need to know how to change it.
My background

Over 20 years working with complex difficulties.

For more than two decades, I worked in residential mental health and addiction services — learning, from the ground up, how to build relationships with people who had experienced severe developmental trauma. Many of the people I worked with were scared of connection, distrustful of others, and carrying patterns that made complete sense given what they'd been through.

That experience taught me something essential: understanding what's wrong isn't enough. People need to know how to change it.

Alongside that work, I trained as a counsellor and immersed myself in attachment theory, trauma theory, and the work of theorists like Daniel Stern and Peter Fonagy — studying how early relationships shape our nervous systems, how we learn to protect ourselves, and how those protections, once useful, can later trap us in patterns that no longer serve us.

How I work

Relational. Trauma-informed. Practically focused.

My approach rests on a belief that a person is a social, embodied, meaning-making system — shaped by relationships, carried in the body, and capable of genuine change through relationship.

That shapes everything about how I work. I don't see the therapeutic relationship as simply the context for change — it is the medium of change. What happens between us in the room matters as much as anything we talk about.

In practice, this means I'm working on several levels at once: what's happening in the body and nervous system, which relational patterns are active, what emotional experience is present, and what meaning someone is making of their situation.

"One criticism I've heard of therapy is: 'I understand what's going on — but what do I do with it?' That's a fair challenge. Insight matters, but it isn't enough on its own."

My focus is on what actually shifts something: developing the capacity to regulate, recognising patterns as they happen, and gradually building a different relationship with yourself and others that you can actually feel, not just describe.

I don't work to a fixed number of sessions. We work at your pace, review regularly, and the work is shaped by what feels most pressing and useful to you.
